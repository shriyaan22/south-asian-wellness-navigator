Understanding and Expressing Distress

       

In many cultures, including South Asian, emotional distress is often expressed through **physical or social symptoms** rather than explicit emotional language (e.g., "my heart feels heavy" instead of "I am depressed"). These are called **Idioms of Distress** and they are valid ways of communicating suffering. Below are two sections to help bridge language gaps.

       

1. Examples of Idioms of Distress

This section is currently a placeholder for a list of common physical/social expressions of distress in South Asian communities (e.g., *dil ghabrana* - heart palpitations/anxiety, *tanaav* - tension/stress).

       
           
               

Dil Ghabrana (Heart Pounding)

               

Literal: Heart palpitations, anxiety, nervousness.

               

Understood as: A somatic expression of high anxiety or panic.

           
           
               

Tanaav / Tension

               

Literal: Stress, high pressure, muscle stiffness.

               

Understood as: General mental or emotional overload, common in conversation.

           
       

2. Widening Emotional Language (Emotional Granularity)

Developing **Emotional Granularity** means having a broad and specific vocabulary to identify your feelings (e.g., distinguishing between *frustration*, *anger*, and *rage*). This skill helps you understand your needs and communicate them more clearly, especially when dealing with family or mental health professionals.

A tool often used for this is an emotional feeling wheel:

[Image of the emotional granularity wheel]

Tips for Expanding Emotional Language:

  1. Name it to Tame it: When you feel distress, try to pause and pick the most precise word you can from a feeling wheel.
  2. The "I Feel" Statement: Use sentence structures like "I feel **[Emotion]** when **[Event]** happens because I need **[Need]**."
  3. Move Past Physical Symptoms: If you feel "tired" or "heavy," ask yourself, "If this tiredness were an emotion, what would it be?" (e.g., lonely, drained, bored).
