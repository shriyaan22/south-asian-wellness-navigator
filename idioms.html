Understanding Idioms of Distress

       

In many South Asian communities, emotional or psychological distress is often expressed through physical symptoms (somatic complaints) or cultural metaphors (idioms). Understanding these idioms is key to bridging communication gaps in health conversations.

       
                       
               

Dil Ghabrana (India/Pakistan)

               

"My heart is restless/fluttering."

               

Often expresses **anxiety, worry, or fear** about the future or a current difficulty.

           
           
               

Jigar Jalna (India/Pakistan)

               

"Burning liver."

               

A common expression for **intense emotional pain, grief, or suffering** caused by betrayal or deep sorrow.

           
           
               

Tension (North India)

               

"Tension."

               

Can describe a wide range of psychological distress, from **mild stress and worry** to **severe depression or anxiety**.

           
           
               

Sir Dukhna (Pakistan/North India)

               

"Head ache/head pain."

               

Frequently used to describe **overwhelming worry, stress, or sadness**, where the emotional burden manifests as a physical headache.

           
           
               

Pet Dukhna (South India)

               

"Stomach ache/pain in the abdomen."

               

Often expresses **underlying fear, nervousness, or social anxiety**, particularly in situations of conflict or major life changes.

           
           
               

Dimag Kharab (India/Pakistan)

               

"Mind is bad/spoiled."

               

Can refer to a range of issues from **feeling mentally tired or stressed** to describing **serious mental illness** (often stigmatized).

           
           
               

Mann Bhalo Na (Bengal/Bangladesh)

               

"My mind/spirit is not good."

               

A nuanced way of saying one is **feeling depressed, uneasy, or generally unwell** emotionally, often related to social or spiritual disruption.

           
           
               

Hikka Dukhna (Punjabi) 

               

"My breastbone/chest hurts."

               

A somatic expression for **heartache or deep sadness**, often related to family conflict or loss.

           
           
               

Sarir Bhalo Na (Bengali)

               

"My body is not good."

               

A common way to describe **generalized malaise or feeling unwell**, serving as a proxy for psychological or emotional exhaustion.

           
           
               

Nerves (Filipino/Desi Diaspora)

               

"My nerves."

               

In the diaspora, often a simplified term for **stress, panic attacks, or anxiety**, reflecting the physical feeling of psychological tension.

           
           
               

Chinta (Hindi/Nepali/Bengali)

               

"Worry/Concern."

               

Used as a primary descriptor for **anxiety related to obligations, finances, or family reputation**.

           
           
               

Manashik Chap (Bengali)

               

"Mental Pressure."

               

Directly translates to **psychological pressure or stress**, especially common regarding academic or professional failure.

           
           
               

Gussa (Hindi/Urdu)

               

"Anger."

               

While literally meaning anger, it can sometimes be used to describe **frustration or helplessness** when one feels unable to solve a problem.

           
           
               

Mata bayak thiynawa (Sinhalese)

               

"I have a fear (or a scary thing)."

               

Used to describe **intense anxiety or panic**, often tied to a specific perceived threat.

           
           
               

En nenjam summa irukka maatenguthu (Tamil)

               

"My heart/mind refuses to stay still."

               

**Restlessness, constant worrying, agitation**, or racing thoughts.

           
           
               

Thalai suthudhu (Tamil)

               

"My head is spinning."

               

**Anxiety, overwhelm, panic-like sensations**, or dizziness due to stress.

           
           
               

Ullae oru aal vidala (Tamil)

               

"Someone inside won't leave me alone."

               

**Intrusive thoughts, rumination**, or overwhelming internal self-criticism.

           
           
               

Manasu valikkuthu (Tamil)

               

"My mind is hurting."

               

**Emotional pain, deep sadness, or distress.**

           
           
               

Satham illaana idathula kooda irukka mudiyala (Tamil) 

               

"I can’t tolerate silence."

               

**Anxiety, internal agitation**, or inability to be alone with one's thoughts; a form of avoidance.

           
           
               

Nazar lagna (General)

               

"To be struck by the evil eye."

               

A cultural explanation for **sudden misfortune, illness, or depression**. Often a way to explain suffering without admitting personal fault or mental illness.

           
       
       
       

Beyond Idioms: Cultivating Emotional Granularity

       

While idioms help bridge cultural gaps, research suggests that developing **emotional granularity** (the ability to describe feelings with high precision) can improve mental health outcomes by allowing for more targeted coping strategies. Use the Feelings Wheel below to practice identifying your emotions beyond just "stress" or "tension."

       
           

Feelings Wheel

           

Tips for Expanding Emotional Language:

  1. **Name it to Tame it:** When you feel distress, try to pause and pick the most precise word you can from the feeling wheel.
  2. **The "I Feel" Statement:** Use sentence structures like "I feel **[Emotion]** when **[Event]** happens because I need **[Need]**."
  3. **Move Past Physical Symptoms:** If you feel "tired" or "heavy," ask yourself, "If this tiredness were an emotion, what would it be?" (e.g., lonely, drained, bored).

This practice can help you move from a vague somatic complaint (like a headache) to a clear psychological need (like the need for validation or rest).
